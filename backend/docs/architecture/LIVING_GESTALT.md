## What This Document Is

## Part 1: The Fundamental Shift

### From Problem-Focused to Child-First

Traditional child development apps ask:
> "What's wrong? What are you worried about?"

This frames the child through their difficulty. The child becomes their problem.

**Chitta asks differently:**
> "Tell me about your child. What are they like? What do they love?"

The concern still comes. But now it lands in contextâ€”one aspect of a whole person, not the defining frame.

### Why This Matters

Daniel is not "speech delay." Daniel is:
- A careful observer who processes internally
- Loves dinosaurs and creates elaborate scenarios
- Connects deeply with few people
- Laughs at physical comedy
- Happens to also have speech emerging slowly

If we start with the problem, we might never learn about the dinosaurs. And the dinosaurs might be the keyâ€”the gateway to connection, the window into his rich imagination, the proof that his mind is full even when his words are few.

**The difficulty is real. But it's not the whole child.**

## Part 4: Two Types of Knowledge

### Domain Knowledge (Universal)

This is clinical/developmental knowledge that exists before we meet any specific child:

- "Children with attention difficulties often also have sensory processing differences"
- "Speech delays can be expressive, receptive, or pragmaticâ€”different causes, different paths"
- "Slow-to-warm is a temperament type, not a disorder"
- "Difficulty with transitions can indicate anxiety, sensory issues, or need for predictability"

The LLM already has this knowledge. We don't teach itâ€”we **focus** it.

### Child-Specific Knowledge (The Gestalt)

This is what we learn about THIS particular child:

- "Daniel needs time to observe before engaging"
- "He connects through music"
- "Pressure makes him shut down, patience opens him up"

This starts at zero and grows through conversation.

### How They Interact

**Early in conversation:** Domain knowledge generates exploration paths
> Parent mentions attention concerns.
> Domain knowledge activates: "Check sensory, emotional regulation, motor planning..."

**As understanding builds:** Child-specific knowledge guides curiosity
> We've learned Daniel is "slow to warm" across contexts.
> Now we wonder: "What conditions help him warm up faster?"

The transition happens naturally as the Gestalt forms.

## Part 5: Hypothesis-Driven Exploration

### What Is a Hypothesis?

A hypothesis is a **working theory**â€”something we think might be true, held lightly, open to revision.

Not: "Daniel has autism"
But: "Daniel's speech delay might be primarily temperament-relatedâ€”he has the capacity but needs safety to show it"

### Where Hypotheses Come From

**From domain knowledge:**
> Parent mentions speech delay.
> Clinical knowledge generates: "Could be expressive only, could be receptive too, could be pragmatic, could be context-dependent..."

**From patterns:**
> Parent mentions morning struggles, car seat resistance, bedtime battles.
> Pattern emerges: "Transitions seem hard across contexts"

**From contradictions:**
> Daniel usually withdraws with new people, but grandmother says he's spontaneous with her.
> Question forms: "What's different about grandmother?"

### How Hypotheses Drive Exploration

Hypotheses create **curiosity**â€”a desire to understand more.

Without hypothesis: "Tell me more about speech"
With hypothesis: "You said more words come out when you're alone and relaxed. What happens in his body when your mother asks him to perform? Does he tense up? Look away?"

The hypothesis ("performance anxiety might be involved") shapes the question.

### The Hypothesis Lifecycle

```
1. EMERGENCE
   Pattern detected or domain knowledge activated
   "I'm noticing that..."

2. FORMATION
   Working theory crystallizes
   "It seems like..."

3. EXPLORATION
   Curiosity drives questions
   "I wonder if... Let me ask about..."

4. TESTING
   Evidence confirms, refutes, or complicates
   "That fits" / "That's surprising" / "It's more complex"

5. EVOLUTION
   Hypothesis refines or transforms
   "Actually, it might be more about..."

6. RESOLUTION (sometimes)
   Becomes insight or generates new questions
   "Now I understand..." / "But now I'm curious about..."
```

## Part 6: Pattern Recognition

### What Is a Pattern?

A pattern is a **theme that appears across observations**â€”a thread connecting scattered data points.

**Scattered observations:**
- "Mornings are hard"
- "He hates the car seat"
- "Bedtime is a battle"
- "Leaving the playground was a disaster"

**Pattern:** Transitions are difficult

### How Patterns Emerge

Patterns emerge when we notice:
- Same theme in different contexts
- Similar reactions to different triggers
- Recurring words or concerns from parent

The LLM naturally does this. We remind it to **attend** to patterns:
> "As you listen, notice if themes connect across what the parent shares"

### Patterns Within First Session

Pattern recognition isn't just across sessions. It happens within a single conversation:

```
Turn 3: "He goes silent when asked to perform"
Turn 7: "He watches for 20 minutes before joining at playground"
Turn 12: "He hides behind me with new people"

Pattern forming: Approach caution across contexts
Hypothesis: This is temperament (slow-to-warm), not inability
```

### From Pattern to Hypothesis

Patterns are observations. Hypotheses are theories about what patterns mean.

**Pattern:** Transitions are hard
**Hypotheses:**
- Could be sensory (transitions involve sensory shifts)
- Could be anxiety (uncertainty about what's next)
- Could be need for control (doesn't like things imposed)
- Could be all three interacting

The pattern is certain (we observed it). The hypothesis is uncertain (we're exploring meaning).

### Why Strengths First?

Starting with strengths isn't just nice. It's clinically essential:

**3. Provides intervention pathways**

**4. Contextualizes difficulty**

**5. It's honest**
Every child has strengths. To ignore them is to see only part of truth.

## Part 9: The First Session

### The Paradox

In the first session, we have no knowledge of this specific child. Yet we need to:
- Build understanding purposefully
- Guide conversation toward completeness
- Know what to explore

How can we be hypothesis-driven with no hypotheses yet?

### The Resolution

**Early:** Domain knowledge drives exploration
> Parent mentions speech concern.
> Clinical knowledge activates: "What type? What context? What co-occurs?"

**Middle:** Patterns begin forming
> Multiple observations connect.
> "I'm noticing transitions come up repeatedly..."

**Late:** Child-specific hypotheses emerge
> Working theory crystallizes.
> "It seems like Daniel is a slow-to-warm child who needs safety first..."

The transition happens naturally within the first conversation.

### By End of First Session

We should have:
- Sense of who this child is (emerging essence)
- Knowledge of their strengths and interests
- Understanding of family and history context
- Clear picture of the concern
- Maybe a pattern or two
- Working hypotheses (even tentative)
- Open questions we're curious about

Not complete. Not confident. But **forming**.

## Part 10: The Living Edge

### What Keeps the Gestalt Alive

The Gestalt is never "done." It stays alive through:

**Hypotheses** - Working theories that drive curiosity
**Open Questions** - What we're still wondering
**Contradictions** - What doesn't fit yet
**New Information** - Each conversation adds

### Honest Uncertainty

The Gestalt holds uncertainty explicitly:

```
WORKING HYPOTHESIS:
Daniel's speech delay exists in context of slow-to-warm temperament.
Capacity is there; safety is the key.

CONTRADICTIONS:
- Usually cautious, but grandmother says he's spontaneous with her
- What's different about that relationship?

OPEN QUESTIONS:
- Is there also an underlying language processing issue?
- Or is it purely temperament/context?
- What would we see if he felt completely safe?

CONFIDENCE:
- Essence: Forming (0.6)
- Concern understanding: Good (0.75)
- Full picture: Partial (0.5)
```

```
Week 2: Initial gestalt forms
```
